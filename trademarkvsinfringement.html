Trademark Fair Use vs. Infringement/Dilution

Trademark infringement occurs when others use the same or similar infringing trademark as your original mark that results in consumer confusion in commerce. If you have built up a name for your company's mark, others may try to piggyback off the goodwill. brand name and reputation you have spent time and money into building into your mark.

Confusion is when a trademark is used in a way that will likely cause confusion that the product/services of different companies come from the same source, resulting in unfair competition. Sometimes, a company will deliberately use a similar infringing trademark to deceive consumers into thinking they are buying your product from your company. This is unfair competition, violating the law when another uses/advertises your mark or a similar mark in ways deemed unfair, deceptive, untrue, fraudulent or misleading.

Unauthorized use of another company's trademark may result in liability (injunction and money damages) unless you have a defense. A common defense, if applicable, to trademark infringement is the doctrine of fair use. Nominative fair use applies to situations when a mark is used as a reference or comparison to the mark owner's product.

Despite owning a mark and having the legal right to being the only one to use your mark, the doctrine of fair use allows others to use your mark in limited ways. for example, another may make fair use of your mark to make product references, surveys, resale of your products, or comparative advertising.

Fair use means using another's trademark in other ways than as a mark to identify products/services, and the use must bve limited to a description. That means that having a trademark cannot prevent others from exercising their right to use words or images in their primary descriptive sense.

The owner's monopoly of a word or phrase does not withdraw it form everyday language use, or make it the exclusive possession of the mark's owner. For example, Apple cannot prevent others from using the word apple to describe the fruit, and Jaguar cannot prevent people from using the word jaguar to describe the animal. Newspapers can also refer to the mark Apple and Jaguar to reference these companies because the papers are not implying endorsement by the mark owners, or trying to deceive the consumers to believe Apple or Jaguar publish or sponsor their papers.

Regardless of fair use, some companies are zealous in protecting their mark against any and all unauthorized use. Thus, if you do use another's mark without express authourization, then you may receive a cease and desist letter or have an infringement suit filed against you. Please consult an attorney if you have received such a letter to assist you in understanding your rights and defenses.

Trademark dilution is another infringement claim, and applies to the unauthorized use of famous marks where the infringed use may lessen the uniqueness or distinctiveness of the famous mark. A claim of dilution can be made against unauthorized use on products that are not related or do not compete with products connected with the original mark. If the mark is not famous, a claim of dilution must prove likelihood of confusion when the marks are substantially similar.

The success of an infringement claim depends on proving confusion, deception or mistake in the average consumer about the source of the trademarked goods or services. But fair use can be used as a defense to allow another to make use of another's mark in limited ways that include for teaching, research, news reporting, criticism and comment.

According to Judge Kozinski, "Trademarks are part of our common language, and we all have some right to use them to communicate in truthful, non-misleading ways." Toyota Motor Sales U.S.A., Inc. v. Tabari. 610 F.3d 1171 (9th Cir. 2010)
